## Task 1 — Is Japan Still an Attractive Market?

*Four questions from deck [[MRA-Day24-M.pdf|slide 10]].*

### Q1.1 — What makes Japan attractive for European companies?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **World's 3rd-largest economy**, after the US and PRC → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=8|p.8]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]].
- **High GDP per capita ≈ $43,235 (2019)** → a market that holds **high profit margins**, especially for foreign investors in consumer goods → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]].
- **Functional infrastructure, reliable legal system, long-established business culture** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]].
- **A high-income, broadly middle-class society**: avg. annual income $40,573 (2019); most consumers can be regarded as middle-class → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=12|p.12]].
- **EU–Japan Free Trade Agreement (in force 1 Feb 2019)** removes tariffs/barriers: once fully implemented Japan scraps customs duties on **97% of goods imported from the EU**; ~**€1 bn** of duties paid annually by EU exporters removed; annual trade could rise by **~€36 bn**; opens key **food & drink** access to 127 m people → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]].
- FTA also **protects designations of origin**, giving EU premium products **premium positioning** in Japanese sales channels → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]].
- **Strong affinity for European lifestyle, design and quality**: Japanese consumers have a high affinity for European lifestyle/design and are attracted to imported "special" products such as Swiss watches and French wines → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=15|p.15]].
- In areas Japan is *not* traditionally competitive (**European wine, cheese, high fashion**), consumers are unlikely to perceive a threat and are **more likely to accept imports** as status symbols → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]].
- **A benchmark market**: satisfying demanding Japanese customers pushes the firm to improve service/quality everywhere — *"If you can sell here, you can sell anywhere"* → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]].

**Insight** — Attractiveness is about **profitability and positioning, not volume**: a wealthy, quality-hungry, pro-European customer base, now made cheaper to reach by the FTA, rewards **premium, high-quality European brands** rather than mass/low-cost plays.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Enter with a **premium, quality-led, "authentically European" positioning** and exploit the **FTA tariff/origin advantage** in categories where Europe is trusted (food, drink, design, fashion).

### Q1.2 — What are the biggest risks of entering Japan?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **A shrinking consumer market**, year on year, driven by an aging society and low birth rate → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=8|p.8]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]].
- **Strong domestic competition in every industry** — at least one well-established Japanese competitor per category; incumbents have known the market and customers **for decades, if not centuries** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=8|p.8]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]].
- **A highly saturated market** — market share can only be gained by **taking it from competitors** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]].
- **Extremely high consumer expectations** — savvy consumers with very high service/product-quality demands who **quickly change providers** if expectations are unfulfilled → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]].
- **Low switching cost / weak brand loyalty** — consumers change brands readily and **always have a reliable Japanese alternative** to fall back on → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=15|p.15]].
- **"Japanese consumer behaviour and attitudes must be taken very seriously"** when attempting entry → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=8|p.8]].
- **Zero tolerance for defects** — small product flaws (e.g. scratches) acceptable elsewhere can **ruin a foreign product's image forever**; most foreign manufacturers run **extra quality checks only for Japan** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]].
- **Slow, resource-heavy entry** — successful entrants report it takes **~3× as long** as entering other markets → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]].
- **COVID-intensified pressure** — the retail market was shrinking and the pandemic intensified competition as buyers became **more selective on value and service** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]].

**Insight** — The core risk is a **saturated, shrinking, incumbent-dominated arena with unforgiving customers**: a foreign entrant must win share from entrenched players who set a world-leading quality/service bar, with little margin for error and a slow payback.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Budget for a **long, well-resourced entry**, hold **flawless quality/after-sales**, and compete on a **differentiated niche** rather than head-on with incumbents.

### Q1.3 — How do demographic changes affect market opportunities?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **Population declining ~379,000 people per year**; could drop below **100 m by 2049**; households projected to fall from 53.33 m (2015) to 50.76 m (2040) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]].
- **65+ = 28.4% of the population (35.89 m) in 2019 — a record high**; 75+ reached 18.5 m → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]]. By **2036, one in three** people will be 65+ → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=11|p.11]].
- **Working-age (15–64) = 59.5% — lowest since records began in 1950**; working-age population expected to **shrink 40% by 2050** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=11|p.11]].
- **Children (0–14) = 12.1%, lowest on record**; the elderly have **outnumbered children since 1997** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=11|p.11]].
- **Wealth is concentrating in the old**: in 2020, **72% of financial assets held by those 55+**, rising to **79% by 2040**; the elderly hold about **half of Japan's $17 trillion** in financial assets → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=27|p.27]].
- **Silver-market opportunity**: four sectors benefit from rising senior numbers — **living/nursing care, food, medical/health, out-of-home services** — worth nearly **¥9 trillion (~$82.9 bn)** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=28|p.28]].
- **Rise of single-person households** — nearly **35% of all households** and increasing; expected to become the **largest consumer group within two decades** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=42|p.42]].
- **Downside of the wealth shift**: by 2030 **~$2 trillion** of financial assets will be held by people with **dementia**; **inheritance tax up to 55%** means family wealth **won't cascade** to younger generations as it does in Europe → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=27|p.27]].
- **A structural consequence**: the shrinking base **intensifies the battle for market share**, which **strengthens consumer power** and forces the world-leading standard of Japanese customer service/adaptability → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=11|p.11]].

**Insight** — Demographics both **shrink the total pie and redistribute it**: fewer, older, wealthier consumers plus a surge of solo households. The opportunity moves **away from "the mass market" toward the silver market and singles**, where the money and the growth actually are.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Target **where wealth and growth concentrate — seniors and single-person households** — rather than the shrinking mainstream family market (developed in Task 3).

### Q1.4 — Is Japan still a growth market or mainly a mature market?

**Evidence — mature / saturated / declining**
- **Fully saturated and continuously shrinking** market because of fast aging → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]].
- **"Japan will continue to be a market with a shrinking consumer base."** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=12|p.12]].
- Japan became a **post-industrial consumer society by the beginning of the 1990s** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=17|p.17]].

**Evidence — pockets of growth within a mature market**
- **Cashless/e-payment**: ~**4.1% annual growth**; plausibly one of the few **booming industries** of Japan's near future → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=20|p.20]].
- **Luxury goods**: pre-COVID the market was expected to **grow ~5.7% in 2020** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=21|p.21]].
- **Functional food**: market ≈ **¥300 bn**, up **38.6% since 2018** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=44|p.44]]; **organic food** shows "great potential" (Japan is the 7th-biggest market) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=43|p.43]].
- **Silver market and single-person households** are the structurally **growing consumer segments** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=28|p.28]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=42|p.42]].

**Insight** — Japan is **mainly a mature, saturated market — not a growth market at the aggregate level**. Growth exists only in **specific segments (silver, singles) and categories (cashless, health/functional food, ethical/organic, luxury)**. Success = **taking share** from incumbents inside those pockets, not riding overall expansion.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Treat Japan as a **share-capture, niche-growth market**: pick a **growing segment/category** and win it on quality and fit, don't count on a rising tide.

### Task 1 — Bottom line

Japan **remains attractive but only on the right terms**: a wealthy, quality-driven, pro-European, FTA-advantaged market ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]]) that is simultaneously **shrinking, aging, saturated and unforgiving** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]]). It is **mature, not growth**, so a European SME should enter as a **premium, differentiated, patient player** aiming at the **segments where wealth and growth concentrate — seniors and singles** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=27|p.27]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=42|p.42]]).

## Task 2 — Understanding Japanese Consumers

*Four questions from deck [[MRA-Day24-M.pdf|slide 11]].*
*(Report distinction: **attitudes** = preferences/expectations & how decisions are made; **behavior** = what consumers actually buy, when, where and how → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=14|p.14]].)*

### Q2.1 — What makes Japanese consumers different from Western consumers?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **World-leading quality expectations** — quality must permeate *every* aspect of a product with continuous improvement; expectations sit far higher on **manufacturers** than on retailers → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=14|p.14]]. Aesthetics matter too — "good design is everywhere" → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=15|p.15]].
- **World-leading service expectations** — the concept of service differs from the West: greeted by 1–2 clerks, near-zero waiting, extensively trained staff; consumers **react to "bad service" very quickly** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=15|p.15]].
- **Brand-conscious but *not* brand-loyal** — brands strongly influence the decision and buyers favour well-known/reliable manufacturers & *keiretsu*, yet **prefer to change brands** (e.g. luxury-car buyers switch brand almost every purchase, unlike Europe) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=15|p.15]].
- **Appetite for novelty** — willing to pay a lot for new/exciting products (*shinhatsubai*, "new release") and for **limited/seasonal special editions** (*gentei*, e.g. KitKat's many flavours) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]].
- **Highly technology-oriented** — a gadget society that expects **state-of-the-art tech**; more new product launches than other developed markets → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]].
- **Selective ethnocentrism** — generally assume Japanese products are higher quality, but readily accept imports where Japan is *not* competitive (**wine, cheese, high fashion**); *"necessity trumps ethnocentrism"* → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]].
- **Shift from group- to individual-oriented consumption** — collectivistic in the past, but since the 1990s recession consumers seek their **own style** and unique products (fuels the second-hand market); yet **new products still diffuse fast** once early adopters accept them → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=17|p.17]].
- **Avid information-seekers** — social media is now the **#1 source** (Twitter ~48 m, Instagram ~33 m Japanese users); they follow influencers and even **check a product's resale value on Mercari before buying it new** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=17|p.17]].
- **Shopping as a leisure activity** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=17|p.17]]; grocery habit of **buying fresh daily** and demanding **small quantities/packaging** (many senior & one-person households) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=18|p.18]].
- **Cash-loving** — **~80% of purchases made by cash**; low crime, ubiquitous ATMs and **mistrust of cashless systems** keep cash appealing; interestingly, **younger consumers use cash more**, the oldest lean on credit cards → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=19|p.19]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=20|p.20]].
- **Low e-commerce penetration (pre-COVID)** — just **8.7% in 2019**, far behind the UK (35.3%), South Korea (22.2%) and China → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=18|p.18]].

**Insight** — The defining difference is a **maximalist standard for quality and service combined with low loyalty and high novelty-seeking**: consumers demand the best, switch easily, research obsessively, and love the new — while retail habits (cash, daily fresh shopping, small packs) remain distinctly local.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Win on **flawless quality + service and a steady drumbeat of new/limited editions**, marketed **digitally through social/influencers**, while **localizing retail mechanics** (cash & konbini payment, small pack sizes).

### Q2.2 — Which consumer attitudes would require foreign firms to adapt?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **Zero-defect quality** — small flaws that pass elsewhere can **ruin a foreign product's image forever**; most foreign makers run **extra QC only for Japan**; quality must be **consistent, always** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]].
- **Best-in-world service / after-sales** — a common expectation for Japanese firms but often **not delivered by foreign producers**; entrants must match it → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]].
- **Weak loyalty + quick switching** — attitudes that mean a firm must **continuously earn** the customer, not bank on retention → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=15|p.15]].
- **Novelty & seasonality expectation** — need a pipeline of **new releases (*shinhatsubai*) and limited/seasonal editions (*gentei*)** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]].
- **Product adaptation is a major success factor** — adapt to local tastes and **strict import regulations**; a key choice is whether to **localize packaging/branding/marketing or keep the "foreignness"** (which may confine sales to special outlets) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]].
- **Packaging/size** — demand for **very small quantities/packaging** driven by senior & one-person households → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=18|p.18]].
- **Information & channel behavior** — heavy **social-media research and influencer reliance**, plus **resale-value awareness (Mercari)** → firms must manage digital presence and second-hand image → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=17|p.17]].
- **Payment behavior** — **cash preference** means offering cash / cash-on-delivery / **convenience-store payment** options (58.6% of online buyers paid by credit card, 34% COD, 25% at a konbini) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=18|p.18]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=19|p.19]].
- **Underlying mindset — "The Customer is God!"** — entrants must **provide a similar level of service quality**; Japanese consumers **do not forgive** bad service, defects or inconvenience → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]].
- **Prepare for a long, changeful entry** — entry takes **~3× longer**; firms must "**be ready for change**" and adapt business processes → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=46|p.46]].

**Insight** — Adaptation is **non-optional and multi-dimensional**: product (quality, size, formulation), service (after-sales), commercial (packaging/branding, payment, channel) and organisational (patience, process change). The unforgiving, low-loyalty attitude removes any margin for a "good enough" foreign standard.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Localize **product, service and payment** to the Japanese bar *before* launch; assume **no second chances** and design an **entry plan ~3× longer** than usual.

### Q2.3 — How did COVID change purchasing behavior?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **Economic shock** — economy shrank at **3.4% annual pace** in early 2020; **~870,000 jobs lost** (mainly hotel, food service, retail); unemployment expected to rise from ~2% to ~4% → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=22|p.22]]. **Consumer confidence collapsed to 43% in Q2 2020** (from 75% in Q1) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=23|p.23]].
- **E-commerce boomed** as stores closed and hours shortened; shopping shifted to **online, delivery and take-out** channels → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=22|p.22]].
- **Online categories jumped** — online shopping for household supplies **+30%**, overall online shopping **+20%**; **cosmetics/beauty, food and daily necessities** (once bought in-store) became the **top-three online categories**; **46%** would continue/increase online clothing purchases → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=24|p.24]].
- **Save-and-restrain sentiment** — **76% altered spending habits to save** on household expenses; office clothes and cosmetics saw the **lowest sales** in summer 2020 → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=22|p.22]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=24|p.24]].
- **Telework rose** and is promoted as "work style reform" → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=22|p.22]].
- **Cashless accelerated** — ~**40%** used cashless payments more frequently; QR-code/contactless seen as a way to **avoid COVID infection** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=20|p.20]].
- **Social media surged** — **72%** had more opportunities to use SNS; **Instagram +65%**, and Instagram is the **most popular channel for discovering products (54%)** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=25|p.25]].
- **Home & wellbeing niches emerged** — stay-home "cozy life," a **pet boom** (Anicom sold **53,456 new policies** Apr–Jun, many first-time owners), and rising sales of **relaxation-oriented beauty/care** products → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=25|p.25]].
- **Ethical/sustainable interest grew** — stronger interest in **food waste and supporting local business** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=24|p.24]].
- **Segment-specific shifts** — **17% of baby boomers shifted to online shopping** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=28|p.28]]; **millennials** cut "unnecessary" spending (novelty, apparel, bags, footwear) and refocused on **groceries, personal care, hygiene** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=33|p.33]]; **women were hit hardest** in the workforce → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=34|p.34]].
- **Luxury stayed physical** — luxury did **not** shift online like other categories, hurting brands that couldn't leverage e-commerce; compounded by the loss of **Chinese tourists** → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=21|p.21]].

**Insight** — COVID **accelerated existing structural shifts** (online/delivery, cashless, social-media discovery, home-centred and health/ethical consumption) and **deepened frugality**, while exposing a **laggard offline channel — luxury**. It didn't create new Japanese traits so much as **fast-forward** them.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Build a **digital-first, delivery-ready, cashless-enabled** go-to-market, lead discovery on **Instagram**, and lean into **home/health/ethical** product angles — the behaviors COVID pulled forward.

### Q2.4 — Which consumer trends are likely to remain after COVID?

**Evidence — the report's own "expected to continue" signals**
- **Telework / work-style reform** — expected to **become prominent after** the COVID crisis → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=22|p.22]].
- **Online / delivery / take-out channels** — a structural, continuing growth in these outlets → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=22|p.22]]; **46%** intend to keep buying clothing online → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=24|p.24]].
- **Digital/cashless payment** — should **receive a boost** and consumers are "**likely to continue** using such convenient services" → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=24|p.24]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=20|p.20]].
- **Health & value consciousness** — with **76%** altering habits to save and rising concern over health, economy and food prices → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=24|p.24]].
- **Ethical / sustainable living** — increasing relevance; seen as a **watershed for regional revitalization** and a new working style among the young → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=24|p.24]].
- **Home-centred & relaxation consumption** — new relaxation-oriented/stay-home patterns **expected to continue**; reinforces the pre-existing structural **cocooning / *sugomori* / "me-time"** trend → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=25|p.25]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=43|p.43]].
- **Social-media–led discovery** — Instagram/SNS entrenched as the product-discovery channel → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=25|p.25]].
- **Pet & wellbeing niches** — new stay-home markets expected to persist (at least until a vaccine) → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=25|p.25]].
- **Likely *not* to fully recover** — **business/office clothing** may face a difficult recovery → [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=22|p.22]].

**Insight** — The **durable** trends are the ones aligned with **pre-existing structural direction**: e-commerce/cashless, home/cocooning, health/ethical consumption and social-media discovery. Purely crisis-driven effects (extreme confidence drop) are temporary; **structurally-reinforced** ones stick.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Bet on the **structural winners** — digital sales, cashless, home & wellbeing categories, ethical positioning, Instagram discovery — and avoid categories tied to the fading office/commute routine.

### Task 2 — Bottom line

### Q3.1 — Which segment offers the greatest opportunity?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **Silver = the biggest wallet.** 65+ are **28.82% (36.23 m)** of the population ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=26|p.26]]) and hold **~72% of all financial assets** and ~half of Japan's **$17 tn** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=27|p.27]]). Four sectors serving them — **living/nursing care, food, medical/health, out-of-home services** — are worth **~¥9 tn (~$82.9 bn)** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=28|p.28]]).
- Silver consumers **prefer high-quality over economical products and will pay more** for quality *and* for ethical products/apparel; they value good after-sales → premium-friendly demand ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=28|p.28]]).
- **Singles = the biggest household base** (~35%) and **spend more freely** than married consumers ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=42|p.42]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=43|p.43]]).
- **Millennials** are the majority-share generation but **frugal, low-asset and premium-resistant** → large but hard to monetise at premium prices ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=31|p.31]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=33|p.33]]).

**Insight** — "Opportunity" = **money × willingness to pay × fit**. On all three, the **silver market leads today**: the largest wealth pool, an explicit preference for quality and ethical goods, and a size that keeps growing.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — The **greatest immediate opportunity is the silver market** — where the spending power and premium willingness actually sit.

### Q3.2 — Which segment is expected to grow?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **Single-person households** are the clear structural grower: **expected to become the largest consumer group in the next two decades, even larger than the silver market** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=42|p.42]]); by 2040 **>9 m** people live alone and **50% of those 15+** will be in single-person households ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=42|p.42]]).
- **Silver market keeps growing** — the number of 65+ will **rise in the coming decades** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=25|p.25]]), and the asset share of 55+ rises **72% → 79% by 2040** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=27|p.27]]).
- **Generation Z grows globally but is muted in Japan** — its relevance is **"not as significant"** as the baby boomer group here ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=37|p.37]]).
- **Millennials** are **declining in spend** — 57% plan to spend less and save more ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=31|p.31]]).

**Insight** — Two segments grow structurally — **singles (toward #1) and silver (sustained)** — and they **overlap** (elderly living alone). Gen Z's global growth story **does not transfer** to demographically-old Japan.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — For the **future** trajectory, bet on **single-person households**; note it **overlaps with the growing silver market** (elderly singles), concentrating the bet.

### Q3.3 — Which segment matches European products best?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **Silver** — a direct fit for **premium European quality + ethical** goods: they prefer high-quality/less-economical products, pay extra for quality and ethics, and value after-sales ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=28|p.28]]). **Female seniors** are increasingly **stylish/fashion-oriented** and eat out with friends/family ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=29|p.29]]).
- **Older (New) Rich** — retain a **preference for luxury fashion and European goods** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=41|p.41]]); Japan is the most relevant luxury-goods market ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=41|p.41]]).
- **Weaker fits:** **millennials** want **simple, relatively cheap but good-quality** products and resist premium ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=33|p.33]]); **younger *Ikina-Rich*** prefer **experiences over luxury goods** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=41|p.41]]).
- **Gen Z** is **brand-conscious and coming to luxury young** (a longer-term fit) but has **little independent income now** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=37|p.37]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=38|p.38]]).
- Backdrop: Japanese consumers broadly have a **high affinity for European lifestyle/design** and accept imports where Japan isn't competitive (**wine, cheese, high fashion**) ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]]).

**Insight** — European products fit best where **willingness to pay for quality and love of European brands coincide** → the **silver market** (quality + ethical premium) and the **older New Rich** (European luxury). The value-driven millennials and experience-seeking young rich are poorer matches for premium goods.

**Strategic implication (generic SME)** — Position for **quality-and-ethics-led premium buyers**: the **silver market** as the core, the **older affluent** as the luxury extension.

### Q4.1 — Which recommendations are most important?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **"The Customer is God!"** is placed first and is reinforced across the whole report: consumers have high purchasing power and **do not forgive** bad service, defects or inconvenience → matching the service/quality bar is a *must* ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]; echoed in the challenges/attitudes chapters, [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=15|p.15]]).
- **"Adapt Your Product!"** is called a **"major factor in successful market entry"**, with the zero-defect quality standard repeated ("small flaws… can ruin the image forever") ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).
- **"Choose the Right Products!"** unlocks the opportunity itself — European high-quality/lifestyle goods have a **"very good sales prognosis"** and high margins ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).
- The remaining three (**Prepare**, **Learn customs**, **Be ready for change**) are **enabling conditions**, not the core value proposition ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]–[[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=46|p.46]]).

**Insight** — The two **make-or-break** recommendations are **quality/service ("Customer is God") and product adaptation** — the same "no second chances / unforgiving consumer" theme that runs through Tasks 1–2. Choosing the right product is the third pillar because it's where European firms already hold an advantage.

**Strategic implication** — Prioritise **flawless quality + service + product fit** above everything; treat preparation, culture and change-readiness as the supporting foundation.

### Q4.2 — Which recommendations are easiest or hardest to implement?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- **Hardest — "Adapt Your Product!"**: requires changing tastes/packaging/branding, meeting **strict import regulations**, running **extra quality checks only for Japan**, and resolving the **localize-vs-keep-foreignness dilemma** (keeping foreignness confines sales to special outlets → smaller reach) ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).
- **Hard — "The Customer is God!"**: delivering **world-leading, ongoing service/after-sales** is operationally demanding ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=15|p.15]]).
- **Moderate — "Prepare Carefully!"**: the **~3× entry time**, patience and resources are a real burden for an SME, but external support (**EU-Japan Centre, chambers**) lowers it ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).
- **Easiest — "Learn Business Customs"** and **"Be Ready for Change"**: mostly **knowledge and mindset**, low direct cost ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=46|p.46]]). **"Choose the Right Products"** is **easy–moderate** because it leverages existing European strengths ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).

**Insight** — There's an **inverse relationship between importance and ease**: the two *most important* recommendations (adapt product, world-class service) are the *hardest and costliest*, while the easy ones are enabling. That's precisely why entry is **slow and resource-heavy**.

**Strategic implication** — Front-load budget and time into the **hard-but-critical** items (product adaptation + service infrastructure); the easy mindset/culture items should be done early but won't, by themselves, win the market.

### Q4.3 — Should European firms standardize or adapt their products?

**Evidence (from the report)**
- The report is explicit: **"Product adaptation is a major factor in successful market entry. Every foreign product needs to be adapted to local tastes and regulations."** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).
- **Regulatory adaptation is mandatory** — strict import regulations force changes in business processes ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).
- **Packaging/size adaptation** — demand for **very small quantities/packaging** (senior & single-person households) ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=18|p.18]]).
- **The nuance — a branding trade-off:** the key question is whether to **adapt packaging/branding/marketing (losing "foreignness")** *or* keep the original form — but keeping foreignness may **confine the product to special outlets** (e.g. foreign supermarkets) and **lose access to larger target groups** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).
- **Foreignness can be an asset** — Japanese consumers have a **high affinity for European lifestyle/design** and buy imports as **status symbols** where Japan isn't competitive ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]]).

**Insight** — The answer is **adapt, not standardize** — but selectively. **Adapt what must be adapted** (quality to the zero-defect bar, formulation to local tastes, packaging/size, regulatory compliance) while **preserving authentic European identity** where that "foreignness" is a premium/status asset. Pure standardization fails the tastes/regulation/size test; pure de-foreignizing throws away a genuine advantage.

**Strategic implication** — **Adapt the product and mix; keep the European brand story.** Match the local bar on quality/size/compliance, but market the authentic European origin to the premium segments.

### Q4.4 — Top three recommendations for an SME entering Japan

*Consolidating Ch. 18 with the Task 1–3 findings into evidence-based **marketing strategies**:*

1. **Position as a premium, authentically-European brand aimed at the silver market (with single-person households as the growth hedge).** European high-quality/lifestyle goods have a "very good sales prognosis" ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]); the silver market has the wealth and the **willingness to pay more for quality and ethics** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=27|p.27]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=28|p.28]]); singles are the **fastest-growing** group ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=42|p.42]]).
2. **Adapt the whole marketing mix to the Japanese bar — never compromise on quality or service.** Zero-defect, consistent product with **extra QC**; **world-class after-sales** ("Customer is God"); **small/localized packaging**; **cash + konbini + cashless** payment options; and **digital, Instagram/influencer-led discovery** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=18|p.18]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=25|p.25]]).
3. **Enter patiently and locally, and keep the product feeling "new."** Budget the **~3× entry time**, **market-test**, and use **EU-Japan Centre / chamber** support ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]); sustain a pipeline of **new releases / limited-seasonal editions (*shinhatsubai* / *gentei*)** to hold the low-loyalty, novelty-seeking consumer ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=16|p.16]]); and **be ready for change** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=46|p.46]]).

### Q5.2 — Is the evidence sufficient to support the conclusions?

**Sufficient for market *orientation*; not sufficient for a *decision*.**

**Where the evidence holds (strengths)**
- **Macro & demographic conclusions are solid** — aging, population decline, wealth concentration rest on official statistics ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=11|p.11]], [[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=27|p.27]]).
- **Broad triangulation** across many independent sources reduces single-source risk.

**Where it doesn't (limitations)**
- **The report disowns its own sufficiency.** Its disclaimer states it *"does not claim to be comprehensive or up to date"* and that *"no person should rely on the contents… without first obtaining advice from a qualified professional person"* ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=56|p.56]]). By its own terms it is **orientation, not decision-grade**.
- **No primary research** → conclusions can't be validated for any specific company or product.
- **Author self-citation / potential confirmation bias** — core behavioral claims lean on the author's own **Haghirian 2010 / 2011** work ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=49|p.49]]).
- **Stale behavioral foundations** — several defining "Japanese consumer" claims cite **1990s–2000s** sources (Schneidewind 1998, Nakano 2007, Schütte & Carliante 1998, Sharma & Shimp 1995) ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=52|p.52]]).
- **Small / opaque samples generalised nationally** — e.g. a **May-2020 survey of n=73** used for online-purchase behavior ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=24|p.24]]); many figures give **no sample size, method, or margin of error**.
- **Aggregator dependence** — heavy use of **Statista / Euromonitor** (second-hand data whose original method isn't shown).
- **Sweeping generalisations** — "Japanese consumers are…" statements flatten a heterogeneous population.
- **Correlation vs causation** — COVID behavior shifts are attributed causally from cross-sectional snapshots.
- **Internal inconsistency** — two different GDP-per-capita figures: **$43,235** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=9|p.9]]) vs **$39,286** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=12|p.12]]).
- **Recency** — as of **2026** the whole report is **~5 years old** and largely **pre-/mid-COVID**; several forecasts are hedged *"until a vaccine is distributed"* ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=25|p.25]]) and the Tokyo Olympics boost it assumed did not materialise as described ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=21|p.21]]).

**Insight** — The evidence is **valid at the macro level but thin at the decision level**: reliable for "should we look at Japan?", inadequate for "should we launch *this product* to *this segment* at *this price*?"

### Q5.3 — What important information is missing?

- **Primary research** — no surveys, interviews, focus groups or observational/ethnographic work with actual consumers.
- **Product-category data** — the report is economy-wide; there is **no category-level demand, size or growth** to scope a specific SME's opportunity.
- **Competitor & price intelligence** — despite naming *"strong competition in every industry"* as the **#1 risk** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]]), it provides **no competitor, market-share or pricing data**.
- **Psychographic / needs-based segmentation** — nothing beyond **age and gender**; no values-, lifestyle- or attitude-based clusters (which the strategy actually depends on).
- **Geographic granularity** — minimal city/prefecture breakdown despite huge Tokyo-metro concentration ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=10|p.10]]).
- **Current / post-vaccine data** — nothing after early 2021; no inflation-era or post-pandemic verification of which trends persisted.
- **Market-entry mechanics** — no distribution, channel-economics, partner or specific-regulation detail.
- **Cost / ROI evidence** — recommendations carry **no costings, metrics or prioritisation** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).
- **Method for the "market test" it recommends** — advised but **undefined** ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=45|p.45]]).

**Insight** — The gaps cluster into **three blind spots: no primary voice-of-customer, no competitor/pricing lens, and no category/geographic granularity** — exactly the inputs an SME needs to convert "Japan is attractive" into a launch plan.

### Q5.4 — If conducting this research today, what would we add?

*Framed with the semester's toolkit — primary research, richer segmentation, and modern/predictive analytics:*

1. **Primary voice-of-customer** — representative **consumer surveys** (with disclosed sample size & confidence intervals), **in-depth interviews**, **focus groups**, and **observational/ethnographic** shopping studies, scoped to the SME's category.
2. **Category & competitor intelligence** — category market sizing, **competitor share/positioning/pricing**, and **retail-audit / POS-scanner** data.
3. **Richer segmentation & trade-off analysis** — **psychographic/behavioral cluster analysis** (beyond age/gender), plus **conjoint** and **willingness-to-pay** studies to price and feature the offer.
4. **Digital & social analytics** — **social listening** (Instagram, LINE, X, Mercari), **search/web analytics**, **sentiment & review mining**, and **e-commerce/marketplace** data — matching the report's own finding that social media is the #1 information source ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=17|p.17]]).
5. **Refreshed, longitudinal data** — current **post-COVID / inflation-era** figures and **tracking studies** to test which 2020 shifts actually stuck.
6. **Geographic analysis** — **prefecture/city-level** segmentation, prioritising Tokyo-metro.
7. **Predictive analytics & test marketing** — **demand forecasting**, **segment-size projection**, **customer-lifetime-value** modelling, and a real **pilot / test-market with A/B price and promotion experiments** before full entry.

**Insight** — In short: **add a primary layer, a competitor/pricing layer, and a modern-analytics layer** on top of this solid secondary base — turning a *market-orientation* document into a *decision-grade* market-research programme.

### Task 5 — Bottom line ★

The report is a **credible, well-triangulated secondary synthesis** — strong on demographics and macro context, genuinely useful for **orienting** a European SME. But **as market researchers we must flag that it is not decision-grade**, and *the report itself agrees*: its disclaimer says it is neither comprehensive nor up to date and should not be relied on without professional advice ([[Day 24-35-japanese-consumers-behavior_0 (1).pdf#page=56|p.56]]). Its evidence is **entirely secondary, age/gender-only, partly stale (1990s–2020 sources, now ~5 years old), self-cited in places, and silent on the competitor, pricing, category and primary-consumer data** an entry decision requires. **Recommendation to the client: treat this report as the *starting map*, then commission the primary, competitor and analytics research above before committing capital.**
